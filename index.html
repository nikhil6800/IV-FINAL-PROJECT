Rainfall in India

About the Rainfall

India is a country that experiences diverse climatic conditions, and rainfall is a crucial aspect of the Indian climate.Monsoon rains dominate the country's weather pattern, with most regions receiving the bulk of their annual rainfall during the monsoon season, which typically lasts from June to September.

India's monsoon season is characterized by two distinct weather systems - the southwest monsoon and the northeast monsoon. The southwest monsoon, which begins in June and lasts until September, brings rainfall to most parts of the country, including the central, northern, and western regions. The northeast monsoon, which occurs from October to December, brings rainfall to the eastern coastal regions of the country.

The amount and distribution of rainfall in India can vary widely from year to year and from region to region, leading to periodic droughts or floods in certain areas. The monsoon is essential to India's agriculture, which is heavily dependent on rainfall, and it also plays a vital role in sustaining the country's water resources and overall economy.

Rainfall over the years in India

Over the last few decades, India has seen a shift in its rainfall patterns. According to the India Meteorological Department (IMD), the country's average annual rainfall has remained relatively constant over the years, but the spatial distribution and intensity of rainfall have changed.

In recent years, some regions have experienced a decrease in rainfall, while others have experienced an increase. For instance, the western coast of India has seen a significant increase in rainfall, leading to severe flooding in some areas. In contrast, regions like central India have experienced a decrease in rainfall, leading to drought conditions.

The year 2019 was a relatively normal year for rainfall, with the country receiving 10% above the long-period average (LPA) rainfall. However, 2020 was an unusual year, with the country receiving 9% less rainfall than the LPA. This rainfall deficit led to drought-like conditions in many regions, affecting agriculture and water resources.

Causes of heavy rainfall in india

Heavy rainfall in India can be caused by several factors, including:

1. Monsoon winds: The monsoon winds play a crucial role in bringing rainfall to India. When these winds are strong, they can cause heavy rainfall in certain regions.

2. Low-pressure systems: Low-pressure systems, such as depressions and cyclones, can cause heavy rainfall in coastal regions and adjacent areas.

3. El Niño and La Niña: El Niño and La Niña are climatic events that can affect rainfall patterns in India. El Niño is associated with lower than normal rainfall, while La Niña is associated with higher than normal rainfall.

4. Urbanization: Urbanization can lead to changes in land use, such as paving over green spaces and building structures, which can alter the local climate and precipitation patterns, leading to increased rainfall in urban areas.

5. Climate change: Climate change is believed to be causing more frequent and intense rainfall events in many regions of the world, including India. As temperatures rise, more moisture is held in the atmosphere, increasing the likelihood of heavy rainfall.
